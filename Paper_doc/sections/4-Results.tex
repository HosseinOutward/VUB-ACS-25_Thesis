\noindent\textbf{Experimental setup.}
We train a ResNet-18 on SVHN in a 5-worker FL setting for 50 rounds using FedAvg aggregation and 5 local epochs per client. Optimization is SGD (learning rate \(10^{-3}\), batch size \(500\)). The learned Wyner--Ziv quantizer (WZQ) will retain at most 5 past reconstructions per worker as side information to save on memory. Quantizers are trained with Adam (learning rate \(10^{-3}\), weight decay \(10^{-4}\)) and a step scheduler (step size \(35\), \(\gamma=0.3\)); batch size \(50{,}000\); for 70 epochs, each epoch sampling a random subset of \(300{,}000\) examples. The initial Gumbel--Softmax temperature is \(1.3\), and the reconstruction-loss weight is \(\lambda=300\).
Note that the nominal \emph{capacity} of WZQ, i.e. number of possible output bins, need not equal the amount actually used; e.g., the initialization quantizer has capacity \(256\) symbols yet activates only 20 for any input values.

\subsection{Rate--Distortion Performance}
Figure~\ref{fig:rate_distortion} compares (i) Combined Protocol, (ii) Temporal-only Protocol, (iii) All-SI Protocol, (iv) learned non-DSC quantizer, and (v) Low-Bit quantizer (sign, Ternary, etc.).
As we can see, all our protocol variants outperform the non-DSC and low-bit baselines, with the temporal-only protocol performing the best, and the all-SI protocol performing the worst among our variants.
We hypothesize that the reason the all-SI protocol performs worse than the temporal-only protocol is a cause of train-inference missmatch: the all-SI protocol learns to recover a reconstruction from a different round which might not be ideal. On the other hand, the temporal-only protocol learns to recover the true gradient update from the same round, even when the side information is more restricted.
We conclude that the temporal correlation is more significant than the inter-worker correlation and the gains from inter-worker SI are not significant enough to justify the train-inference mismatch.

\subsection{FL Performance}
Figure~\ref{fig:fl_performance} reports FL accuracy per round. The trained accuracy is nearly perfect and outperforms the sign quantizer in both accuracy and communication cost. It also outperforms the identity (uncompressed) protocol, probably as the compression acts as a regularizer.
