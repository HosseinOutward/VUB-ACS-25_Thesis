\subsection{Potentials of Distributed Source Coding in Federated/Distributed Learning}

Communication is the dominant bottleneck in federated learning. The data exchanged between workers and the server mainly consists of two parts:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item Model parameters $\theta$, which are sent from the server to the workers for initialization.
    \item Gradients $g$, which are computed locally at the workers and transmitted back to the server for aggregation.
\end{enumerate}

It has been repeatedly demonstrated in the literature that gradients communicated by different nodes are strongly correlated.
This is expected, since these gradients collectively contribute to forming a global model with improved generalization capabilities, so we can assume that they are all generally trending towards the same optimal point.
Given this correlation, the advantages of applying Distributed Source Coding (DSC) to compress gradients become evident; DSC is specifically designed to exploit dependencies among correlated sources, making it a natural fit for distributed learning scenarios.
It's important to note that depending on how you group gradients, the correlation structure could be different, which could be exploited in different ways; in our work, we focus on individual parameters, which is why it's called a Wyner-Ziv Quantizer.

Below we see empirical evidence gathered by other works, showing the correlation between gradients from different workers, grouped either by layer or not grouped (using the entire gradient vector).

Figure \ref{fig:correlation - LGCD-DL - MI per layer} shows the difference between marginal entropy and mutual information (MI) between gradients from 2 workers. This discrepancy (marginal entropy minus MI) represents the information unique to each worker, which is the part that cannot be compressed using DSC. We can see that this difference is relatively small, indicating high potential for compression using DSC. The figure also shows that the unique component is larger in the first and last layers, which is expected since these layers are more sensitive to the specific data batch due to their proximity to the input and output.

    \caption{Mean marginal entropy and the mean MI per layer, averaged over the iterations, for (a) ResNet50 trained on CIFAR-10 and (b) PSPNet trained on CamVid. (Source: \cite{Abrahamyan2022LGC})}
    \label{fig:correlation - LGCD-DL - MI per layer}
\end{figure}

Figure \ref{fig:correlation - LGCD-DL - MI through iterations} shows similar information but across training iterations. We can see that the discrepancy between marginal entropy and MI remains relatively small throughout training, indicating sustained potential for DSC compression across the entire training process. However, this analysis uses information-theoretic measures (MI vs. entropy). When we examine a different correlation measure, Pearson correlation, we observe a different pattern in Figure \ref{fig:correlation - a low complexity}: the correlation starts high at the beginning of training, then decreases as training progresses. This decline occurs because local gradients become increasingly specialized to their local data distributions, representing a form of client drift that reduces the effectiveness of DSC compression.

\subsection{Learned Wyner-Ziv Quantizer in Federated Learning}
\subsubsection{Differences with Past DSC Implementations in Federated Learning}
Existing approaches to gradient compression vary in focus, ranging from compressing the entire model vector (e.g., low-precision quantization or entropy coding of the concatenated gradient) to per-parameter compression through quantization schemes, including binary/ternary quantizers and learned quantizers, with some incorporating DSC-like concepts.

Among works that leverage DSC for quantization, most have focused on classical DSC constructions, which are computationally complex and resource-intensive to design and implement. Our approach differs fundamentally by employing a learned successive Wyner-Ziv quantizer that is trained on-the-fly during the federated learning process. This design choice provides two key practical advantages: (1) it is computationally more efficient than conventional DSC code-design pipelines, eliminating the need for complex syndrome calculations and channel coding, and (2) it adaptively learns the correlation structure from the actual gradient statistics encountered during training, rather than relying on pre-assumed statistical models.
