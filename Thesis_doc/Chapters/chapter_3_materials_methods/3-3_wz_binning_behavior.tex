\section{WZ Binning Behavior on Distributions and Their Compression Gains}

We examine the behavior of the learned \emph{layered} Wyner–Ziv (WZ) quantizer with respect to bin assignments under various source distributions and side-information configurations, and discuss the resulting compression gains.

\paragraph{Gaussian source without side information.}
As a baseline, consider a Gaussian source with no side information. In this setting, the WZ quantizer should reduce to a standard learned quantizer. Figure~\ref{fig:wz_binning_gaussian_no_side_info} confirms this: bin spacings adapt to the local density of the source. The quantizer has three layers with 16 bins per layer but, in practice, utilizes only 20 \emph{combinations} of bins. Increasing the number of \emph{utilized} combinations is a recurring challenge for this model; a mitigation is introduced in the next section. Bins separate cleanly as a function of the input $x$, and the reconstruction error increases as $x$ moves away from the mean (not the median) of the bin-defined subregions. This same model is used for the initial warm-up phase of our protocols (discussed later).

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Chapters/chapter_3_materials_methods/figures/binning beh - basic marginal}
    \caption{Quantizer with three layers and 16 bins per layer, trained on a Gaussian distribution without side information.
    \emph{Top}: bin regions and reconstruction error as a function of input $x$; the three orange lines indicate the per-layer bin reconstruction levels.
    \emph{Bottom}: reconstructed values for assigned bins when conditioned on the side information typically associated with that $x$ (absent in this case).}
    \label{fig:wz_binning_gaussian_no_side_info}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Gaussian source with side information.}
Now consider a Gaussian source with side information
\begin{equation}
    Y = X + N,
\end{equation}
where $N \sim \mathcal{N}(0,\,0.01)$. With informative side information, we expect bins to \emph{enlarge} and be \emph{reused} across multiple input regions, since the decoder can disambiguate reconstructions using $Y$. Figure~\ref{fig:wz_binning_gaussian_with_side_info} shows that bins do indeed expand and are reused across disjoint portions of the input domain.

\begin{figure}[H]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=1\textwidth]{Chapters/chapter_3_materials_methods/figures/binning beh - gaussian}
    \caption{Quantizer with 2 layers and 4 bins per layer, trained on a Gaussian distribution with side information $Y=X+N$, $N\sim\mathcal{N}(0,0.01)$.}
    \label{fig:wz_binning_gaussian_with_side_info}
\end{figure}

Given the multi-stage nature of the quantizer, these effects are more clearly reflected in rate–distortion behavior. Figure~\ref{fig:rate_dist_gaussian_with_side_info} reports the rate–distortion curves with and without side information, where the rate is computed from the learned prior (matching the plot reported by \cite{JoukovskyLayeredWZ}).

\begin{figure}[H]

    \caption{Rate–distortion for a Gaussian source with and without side information. each dot is an independently trained model. Rate is entropy of the prior (more info in section 3.6), reproducing the behavior in \cite{JoukovskyLayeredWZ}.}
    \label{fig:rate_dist_gaussian_with_side_info}
\end{figure}

\paragraph{Uniform source with side information: a failure case.}
Finally, Figure~\ref{fig:uniform_dis_binning} illustrates a case in which the model underperforms. The source is uniform, and the side information is a noisier version of the source (uniform noise with range $0.25$). The quantizer has one layer with only two bins. One would expect near-equal bin spacing (aside from edge effects) with an alternating assignment pattern, but this behavior does not emerge. A plausible explanation is that the Gumbel–Softmax exploration is insufficient to traverse the space of bin configurations; additionally, while the prior term should guide the model toward lower rate, it may not do so quickly when the Gumbel temperature is high. We attempted several remedies, with limited success.
